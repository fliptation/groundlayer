# Core Principles

These principles guide every layer of the Ground Layer system. They're not rules imposed from above — they're design constraints that help us build systems that actually work for people and the planet.

## 1. Decentralize Power

No single entity — government, corporation, or individual — should control the resources that everyone depends on. Power works best when it's distributed.

**In practice:**
- Decisions are made at the smallest effective level
- Infrastructure is owned by the communities that use it
- No single point of failure, no single point of control
- Leadership rotates; stewardship replaces ownership

## 2. Transparency by Default

Trust is built through visibility, not authority. When systems are open to inspection, corruption has nowhere to hide.

**In practice:**
- All processes, budgets, and decisions are publicly documented
- Open data, open books, open meetings
- Privacy is for people; transparency is for institutions
- Anyone can audit, question, and propose improvements

## 3. Regeneration Over Extraction

Every process should give back more than it takes. This applies to soil, to communities, to people's energy and time.

**In practice:**
- Agriculture builds soil instead of depleting it
- Economic activity strengthens communities instead of draining them
- Work replenishes people instead of burning them out
- Systems are designed for long-term health, not short-term profit

## 4. Consent Over Coercion

Participation is voluntary. People cooperate because the system serves them, not because they're forced to. Good design makes the right choice the easy choice.

**In practice:**
- No mandates — incentives and good design instead
- Opt-in participation at every level
- Exit rights are real and accessible
- Persuasion through results, not through force

## 5. Shared Knowledge

Information wants to be free. When knowledge is locked behind paywalls and patents, everyone loses. When it's shared, everyone gains.

**In practice:**
- Research is publicly funded and publicly owned
- Education is free and accessible to all
- Technology is open-source by default
- Traditional and indigenous knowledge is respected and preserved

## 6. Local First, Connected Always

The best solutions come from the people closest to the problem. But local communities are stronger when they're connected to a global network.

**In practice:**
- Solutions are designed locally, shared globally
- Communities maintain autonomy while cooperating on shared challenges
- Shared protocols enable interoperability without uniformity
- Cultural diversity is a feature, not a bug

## 7. Whole-System Thinking

Everything is connected. Food affects health. Energy affects economy. Education affects governance. We design for the whole, not just the parts.

**In practice:**
- Changes in one layer consider impacts on all others
- Cross-disciplinary collaboration is the norm
- Feedback loops are identified and designed for
- Unintended consequences are actively monitored

## How to Use These Principles

These aren't abstract ideals. They're practical filters. When designing any system, process, or institution, ask:

1. Does this concentrate or distribute power?
2. Is this transparent and auditable?
3. Does this regenerate or extract?
4. Is participation voluntary?
5. Is the knowledge being shared or hoarded?
6. Is this locally appropriate and globally connected?
7. Have we considered the whole system?

If the answer to any of these is wrong, redesign until it's right.
