# Get Started

## You Don't Need Permission

The most important thing to understand: **you can start right now, right where you are.** You don't need to wait for a government program, a wealthy donor, or a global movement. Every layer of Ground Layer can begin at the scale of one person, one household, one neighborhood.

## Start With What's Alive

Don't start with what you think you "should" do. Start with what excites you. What layer pulls you in? What problem keeps you up at night? What skill do you have that your community needs?

The system is modular by design. Work on the piece that matters to you. Trust that others are working on the other pieces.

## Five Steps to Begin

### Step 1: Learn

Pick one layer that resonates with you. Read about it. Find examples of people who've already done it. You'll discover that almost nothing here is new — it's all been tried and proven somewhere.

**Resources to explore:**
- The docs in this project (you're reading one now)
- The "Already Happening" section in each layer
- Local organizations already doing this work in your area

### Step 2: Connect

Find the people near you who care about the same things. They're already there — you just haven't met them yet.

**Where to look:**
- Community gardens, cooperatives, mutual aid groups
- Local environmental organizations
- Maker spaces, skill-sharing networks
- Faith communities, neighborhood associations
- Online forums and local social media groups

### Step 3: Map

Understand your local context. Every community is different.

**Questions to ask:**
- What are the biggest challenges here?
- What resources exist that aren't being fully used?
- Who's already doing good work?
- Where is land, food, energy, or knowledge being wasted or hoarded?
- What are the existing strengths of this community?

### Step 4: Act

Start small. Pick one concrete thing you can do in the next week.

**Examples by layer:**

| Layer | One-Week Action |
|---|---|
| Food & Land | Plant something. Visit a farmers market. Start composting. |
| Health | Start a walking group. Cook a meal with neighbors. |
| Governance | Attend a local council meeting. Start a neighborhood group chat. |
| Economy | Join a time bank. Shop at a co-op. Lend a tool. |
| Education | Teach a skill to someone. Host a book discussion. |
| Energy | Audit your energy use. Research community solar options. |

### Step 5: Share

Document what you do. Share what works and what doesn't. This is how the system learns and evolves.

**How to share:**
- Contribute your experience back to this project (see [Contributing](../CONTRIBUTING.md))
- Write about it — blog, social media, community newsletter
- Talk to your neighbors about what you're learning
- Connect with other communities doing similar work

## Common Concerns

### "This is too idealistic"

Every system that exists today was once considered impossible. Universal suffrage, public libraries, the internet, weekends — all were dismissed as utopian fantasies before they became normal. The question isn't whether change is possible. It's whether you'll participate in shaping it.

### "I'm just one person"

Every movement starts with individuals. Rosa Parks was one person. The first community garden was one person with a shovel. Your job isn't to change the world alone. It's to change your corner of it and connect with others doing the same.

### "The system is too powerful to change"

Systems change when the alternatives become more attractive than the status quo. You don't have to fight the old system — you can build the new one. When the new way works better, people naturally shift toward it.

### "I don't have the skills"

You have more than you think. Can you cook? Garden? Listen? Organize a meeting? Fix things? Teach? Care for someone? These are the skills that build commons. You don't need a degree. You need willingness.

## The Bigger Picture

Individual action matters, but systemic change requires collective action. As you begin, look for opportunities to:

- **Federate** — connect with other groups doing similar work
- **Advocate** — push for policies that support commons-based approaches
- **Demonstrate** — show that alternatives work by living them
- **Document** — create knowledge that others can use
- **Contribute** — improve this open-source blueprint for everyone

The path isn't linear. It's a network. The more nodes that light up, the stronger the whole system becomes.

**Start where you are. Use what you have. Do what you can. Share what you learn.**
