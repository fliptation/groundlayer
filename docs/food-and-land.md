# Layer 1: Food & Land

## The Problem

Our food system is broken in ways most people never see. Industrial agriculture depletes topsoil at 10 times the rate it's created. A handful of corporations control the global seed supply. Food travels thousands of miles before reaching your plate. Meanwhile, one-third of all food produced is wasted, and nearly a billion people go hungry.

Land — the foundation of all life — is treated as a speculative asset. Prices rise, communities are displaced, and the connection between people and the earth that feeds them is severed.

## The Alternative

A food and land system designed around abundance, resilience, and stewardship.

### Community Land Trusts

Land is held in common by the community, not owned by speculators. People can use the land, build on it, farm it — but it can't be bought and sold for profit. This keeps housing affordable, farming viable, and communities stable.

**How it works:**
- A community trust acquires land and holds it permanently
- Residents and farmers lease the land at fair rates
- Decisions about the land are made by the people who live on it
- The land can never be sold to developers or speculators

### Open-Source Agriculture

Farming knowledge, seed varieties, and soil data are shared freely — not locked up in corporate patents.

**Key elements:**
- **Shared seed banks** — locally adapted, non-patented seeds available to all farmers
- **Open soil data** — community-maintained databases of soil health, what grows well where
- **Regenerative techniques** — composting, cover cropping, agroforestry, no-till methods shared as open knowledge
- **Peer-to-peer learning** — experienced farmers mentoring new ones

### Local Food Networks

Shorten the distance between where food is grown and where it's eaten. This reduces waste, builds relationships, and keeps money in the community.

**Models that work:**
- **Community Supported Agriculture (CSA)** — people buy shares in a local farm's harvest
- **Food hubs** — shared infrastructure for storage, processing, and distribution
- **Community gardens and urban farms** — growing food where people live
- **Free food forests** — public spaces planted with fruit trees, berry bushes, and herbs

### Regenerative Practices

Move beyond "sustainable" (which just means "not getting worse") to regenerative (actively healing the land).

- Build topsoil instead of depleting it
- Increase biodiversity instead of monoculture
- Capture carbon in the soil instead of releasing it
- Work with natural cycles instead of against them

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Learn to grow something — even a windowsill herb garden connects you to food
- Join or start a community garden
- Buy from local farmers when possible
- Compost your food waste

**As a community:**
- Research community land trusts in your area
- Start a seed library at your local library
- Organize a farmers market or food swap
- Map unused land that could become food-producing

**As a region:**
- Support policies that protect farmland from development
- Fund soil health research and extension services
- Create food policy councils with farmer and community representation
- Invest in local food infrastructure (cold storage, processing facilities)

## Already Happening

This isn't theoretical. Around the world:
- Community land trusts operate in hundreds of cities
- La Via Campesina represents 200 million small farmers globally
- The Open Source Seed Initiative has freed hundreds of seed varieties from patents
- Cuba transformed its food system to local, organic production out of necessity — and thrived
- Detroit's urban farming movement grows millions of pounds of food on formerly vacant lots

The knowledge exists. The models work. What's needed is connection and scale.
