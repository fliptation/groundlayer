# Layer 4: Economy

## The Problem

The current economy is designed to concentrate wealth. It measures success by GDP — a number that goes up whether you're building a hospital or cleaning up an oil spill. It treats unpaid work (raising children, caring for elders, maintaining communities) as worthless. It requires infinite growth on a finite planet.

Money is created as debt by private banks. Inequality compounds generation after generation. Communities that can't compete in the global market are abandoned. And the people who do the most essential work — growing food, caring for the sick, teaching children — are paid the least.

## The Alternative

An economy that serves life, values all contributions, and circulates wealth instead of concentrating it.

### Commons-Based Production

The things everyone needs — water, energy, knowledge, basic infrastructure — are managed as commons, not private property.

**Key elements:**
- **Shared infrastructure** — community-owned internet, energy grids, water systems
- **Open-source tools** — free software, hardware designs, and production methods
- **Cooperative ownership** — businesses owned by the workers who run them
- **Community wealth building** — anchor institutions (hospitals, universities) buy local and hire local

### Local Currencies & Mutual Credit

Communities don't have to wait for national economic policy to work. They can create their own exchange systems.

**Models:**
- **Local currencies** — money that can only be spent within the community, keeping wealth circulating locally
- **Mutual credit** — businesses extend credit to each other without needing banks
- **Community investment funds** — residents pool money to fund local projects
- **Cooperative banks** — financial institutions owned by their members, lending for community benefit

### Time Banking

A radical idea: everyone's hour is worth the same.

**How it works:**
- You spend an hour helping someone — you earn one time credit
- You spend that credit receiving an hour of help from someone else
- A lawyer's hour equals a gardener's hour equals a teacher's hour
- This values care work, community work, and skills that the market ignores

### Circular Economy

Waste is a design failure. In nature, there is no waste — everything is food for something else.

**Principles:**
- Design products to be repaired, reused, and recycled
- Share rather than own (tool libraries, car sharing, equipment pools)
- Local manufacturing with local materials where possible
- Composting and nutrient cycling close the loop on organic materials

### Redefining Wealth

GDP measures activity, not wellbeing. Better measures exist:

- **Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI)** — accounts for inequality, environmental damage, and unpaid work
- **Gross National Happiness** — Bhutan's framework that measures psychological wellbeing, health, education, governance, and ecology
- **Doughnut Economics** — Kate Raworth's framework ensuring everyone has enough without overshooting planetary boundaries

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Support local businesses and cooperatives
- Learn about and join a time bank
- Invest locally when possible — credit unions, community funds
- Reduce, repair, reuse before buying new

**As a community:**
- Start a time bank or local currency
- Create a tool library or sharing collective
- Support or start a worker cooperative
- Map your local economy — where does money flow, and where does it leak out?

**As a region:**
- Support cooperative development with policy and funding
- Implement community wealth building strategies
- Adopt wellbeing indicators alongside GDP
- Create public banking options

## Already Happening

- Mondragon Corporation in Spain is a federation of 80+ cooperatives employing 80,000+ people
- Bristol Pound, BerkShares, and hundreds of other local currencies operate worldwide
- Time banking networks exist in 30+ countries
- Emilia-Romagna in Italy has a cooperative economy that outperforms most capitalist regions
- Amsterdam adopted Doughnut Economics as its official development framework
- Patagonia turned its entire company into a trust that funds environmental work

The economy is not a law of nature. It's a set of rules we wrote. We can write better ones.
