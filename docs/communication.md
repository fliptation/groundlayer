# Layer 11: Communication & Media

## The Problem

A few corporations control most of the world's information flow. Algorithms optimize for engagement — outrage, fear, division — not truth or understanding. Local journalism is collapsing as advertising revenue flows to tech platforms. Misinformation spreads faster than facts.

Communities have lost the ability to tell their own stories. Culture is increasingly manufactured and distributed by a handful of media conglomerates. The public square has been privatized, and the terms of conversation are set by algorithms designed to maximize screen time.

## The Alternative

Communication infrastructure owned by communities, accountable to truth, and designed for understanding.

### Community Media

Locally owned newspapers, radio stations, podcasts, and newsletters. Journalism accountable to the community it serves.

**Key elements:**
- **Community-funded journalism** — supported by readers and listeners, not advertisers
- **Local radio** — low-power FM stations run by volunteers covering local issues
- **Community newsletters** — regular dispatches connecting neighbors to what matters locally
- **Collaborative reporting** — community members contributing to the stories that affect them

### Decentralized Platforms

Federated social networks, community-run forums, and open protocols that no single entity controls.

**Models that work:**
- **Mastodon and the Fediverse** — interconnected, community-run social networks
- **Matrix** — open protocol for real-time communication, self-hostable
- **Community forums** — moderated by members, governed by community standards
- **Open protocols** — email, RSS, and web standards that remain free and open

### Information Commons

Knowledge as a shared resource, not a product. Publicly funded research freely available. Community archives preserving local history.

- Open-access research and publishing
- Community-maintained local history archives
- Open data portals for civic information
- Public libraries as knowledge hubs and community centers

### Media Literacy for All

Critical thinking about information — taught at every age, practiced in every community.

- Understanding how algorithms shape what you see
- Skills for verifying claims and checking sources
- Creating media, not just consuming it
- Recognizing manipulation, propaganda, and misinformation

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Support local journalism — subscribe, donate, contribute
- Move conversations to community-owned platforms
- Practice and teach source verification
- Create and share local stories

**As a community:**
- Start a community newsletter or podcast
- Set up a local Mastodon or Matrix server
- Organize media literacy workshops
- Create a community archive or oral history project

**As a region:**
- Fund local journalism through public grants
- Support community broadband as communication infrastructure
- Require media literacy in education curricula
- Protect freedom of press and open internet access

## Already Happening

This isn't theoretical. Around the world:
- The Fediverse connects millions through decentralized, community-run social networks
- Community radio reaches 500+ million people worldwide
- Wikipedia remains the world's largest open knowledge project, maintained by volunteers
- Taiwan's digital democracy tools combat misinformation through collective intelligence
- ProPublica and other nonprofit newsrooms provide investigative journalism as a public service

The tools exist. The desire for truth persists. What's needed is communities reclaiming their own stories.
