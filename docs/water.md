# Layer 9: Water & Sanitation

## The Problem

Water is the most essential substance for life, yet 2 billion people lack safe drinking water. Water is increasingly privatized and commodified — traded on futures markets like oil. Industrial agriculture and industry pollute aquifers and rivers that took millennia to form.

Climate change intensifies both droughts and floods. Wealthy nations waste water at staggering rates while communities downstream suffer scarcity. Sanitation systems in much of the world are inadequate, causing preventable disease and death. The water cycle itself is being disrupted.

## The Alternative

Water and sanitation managed as commons — protected, shared, and governed by the communities that depend on them.

### Community Water Stewardship

Water managed as a commons by the communities that depend on it. Not owned by corporations, not controlled by distant bureaucracies.

**How it works:**
- Local water boards with democratic governance
- Transparent monitoring of water quality and quantity
- Community control over allocation and pricing
- Traditional and indigenous water management knowledge preserved and applied

### Watershed Protection

Protecting entire watersheds — from mountain springs to river deltas — as living systems.

**Key elements:**
- **Reforestation** — trees as nature's water infrastructure, filtering and regulating flow
- **Wetland restoration** — natural sponges that filter pollutants and buffer floods
- **Riparian buffers** — vegetated zones along waterways that protect water quality
- **Groundwater recharge** — allowing rain to infiltrate rather than run off impervious surfaces

### Open-Source Water Systems

Low-cost, locally buildable systems for water purification, harvesting, and recycling. Plans shared freely, adapted to local conditions.

**Models that work:**
- **Biosand filters** — simple, effective water purification buildable anywhere
- **Rainwater harvesting** — capturing and storing rain for household and community use
- **Greywater recycling** — reusing wash water for irrigation and non-potable needs
- **Solar distillation** — using sunlight to purify water with no energy inputs

### Sanitation as a Right

Everyone deserves dignified sanitation. Decentralized, appropriate systems that turn waste into resources.

- Composting toilets that produce fertile soil
- Constructed wetlands for community wastewater treatment
- Decentralized treatment systems maintained locally
- Waste-to-resource cycles that close the loop

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Learn where your water comes from and how it's treated
- Reduce water waste — fix leaks, use water mindfully
- Install rainwater collection if local laws permit
- Compost and reduce what goes down the drain

**As a community:**
- Protect local waterways — organize river cleanups
- Start a community water monitoring program
- Map your local watershed and its vulnerabilities
- Advocate for water as a public trust, not a commodity

**As a region:**
- Invest in watershed protection and restoration
- Support community water governance
- Fund open-source water infrastructure
- Oppose water privatization

## Already Happening

This isn't theoretical. Around the world:
- Cochabamba's water war reversed privatization and restored community control
- Singapore recycles 40% of its wastewater back into drinking water
- Biosand filters provide safe water for millions in developing countries
- Indigenous water protectors have blocked pipelines threatening watersheds worldwide
- The human right to water is recognized by the United Nations

The knowledge exists. The technology is simple. What's needed is the will to treat water as sacred rather than sellable.
