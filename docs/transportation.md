# Layer 10: Transportation & Mobility

## The Problem

Cities were redesigned around cars, not people. Transportation is the largest source of emissions in many countries. Car dependence isolates the elderly, disabled, and poor — those who can't drive are effectively trapped.

Infrastructure built for cars costs municipalities far more than it generates in tax revenue. We spend hours daily in traffic, alone, burning fossil fuels to move two-ton machines for single occupants. Meanwhile, the most efficient and joyful forms of movement — walking, cycling, public transit — are underfunded and dangerous.

## The Alternative

Mobility systems designed for people, communities, and the planet.

### Walkable Communities

Mixed-use neighborhoods where daily needs are within walking distance. The simplest, cheapest, healthiest transportation is your own two feet.

**Key elements:**
- **Mixed-use zoning** — homes, shops, schools, and workplaces in the same neighborhood
- **Human-scaled streets** — narrow, tree-lined, with wide sidewalks and places to rest
- **Public spaces** — plazas, parks, and gathering spots that invite lingering
- **Accessible design** — sidewalks, crossings, and buildings usable by everyone

### Transit Cooperatives

Community-owned public transit — governed by riders and workers, not distant corporations or underfunded bureaucracies.

**Models that work:**
- **Community bus cooperatives** — routes designed by the people who ride them
- **Bike-share cooperatives** — community-owned and maintained cycling infrastructure
- **Ride-share cooperatives** — driver-owned platforms without corporate extraction
- **Demand-responsive transit** — flexible routes that adapt to community needs

### Shared Mobility

Access over ownership. One shared car replaces 10+ private ones.

- Car-sharing cooperatives and community vehicle pools
- Cargo bike networks for local deliveries
- Tool libraries for vehicles — borrow a truck when you need one
- Community ride-matching for commutes and errands

### Human-Scaled Infrastructure

Infrastructure designed to invite movement, not just motoring.

- Protected bike lanes separated from car traffic
- Pedestrian zones and car-free streets
- Traffic calming — speed bumps, chicanes, raised crossings
- Universal accessibility — mobility is for everyone, regardless of ability

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Walk or bike for trips under 2 miles
- Use public transit when possible
- Share rides when driving is necessary
- Advocate for safer streets in your neighborhood

**As a community:**
- Push for bike lanes and pedestrian zones
- Start a car-sharing cooperative
- Organize a community bike repair workshop
- Map walking and cycling routes and barriers

**As a region:**
- Invest in public transit infrastructure
- Implement mixed-use zoning reforms
- Fund protected cycling networks
- Prioritize transit, walking, and cycling in transportation budgets

## Already Happening

This isn't theoretical. Around the world:
- Amsterdam transformed from car-centric to cycling paradise through citizen activism in the 1970s
- Bogota's TransMilenio and Ciclovia show how cities can prioritize people over cars
- Car-sharing cooperatives in Switzerland and Germany serve hundreds of thousands of members
- Barcelona's superblocks reclaim streets for people, reducing pollution and noise
- Pontevedra, Spain went nearly car-free and saw zero pedestrian deaths in over a decade

The models exist. The benefits are proven. What's needed is the courage to redesign streets for people.
