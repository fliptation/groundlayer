# Layer 8: Technology & Infrastructure

## The Problem

A handful of corporations control the digital infrastructure of modern life — search, communication, commerce, identity. Technology that could liberate is used to surveil and extract. Critical infrastructure depends on proprietary systems that communities can't inspect, modify, or control.

Software we depend on daily can be changed, restricted, or shut down without our consent. Our data — conversations, movements, purchases, relationships — is harvested and sold. Communities have no sovereignty over their own digital lives.

## The Alternative

Technology and infrastructure built by, for, and accountable to the people who use it.

### Open-Source Everything

Software, hardware, and protocols built in the open. Community-governed, auditable, forkable. No critical tool should be locked behind a corporate gate.

**Key elements:**
- **Open-source software** — operating systems, productivity tools, communication platforms owned by no one
- **Open hardware** — repairable devices, open schematics, community-manufactured components
- **Open protocols** — email, web, and communication standards no single entity controls
- **Open data formats** — your data is never trapped in a proprietary silo

### Community Networks

Mesh networks, community broadband, and local ISPs owned and operated by the people who use them. Internet access as a commons, not a product.

**Models that work:**
- **Mesh networks** — decentralized, resilient networks where each node strengthens the whole
- **Community broadband** — municipally or cooperatively owned fiber and wireless
- **Local ISPs** — small, accountable providers rooted in the communities they serve
- **Offline-first design** — tools that work without constant connectivity

### Data Sovereignty

Communities and individuals own their data. Your information stays under your control.

- Local data cooperatives managing community information
- Self-hosted services for email, chat, file storage
- Encryption by default for all communications
- Right to export, delete, and control your own data

### Appropriate Technology

Not every problem needs the latest technology. The best tool is the one that fits the context — low-tech, resilient, and repairable solutions valued alongside high-tech ones.

- Technology assessed for long-term sustainability, not just innovation
- Repair and maintenance prioritized in design
- Local capacity to understand and maintain the tools being used
- Human relationships valued over digital replacements

## Getting Started

**As an individual:**
- Switch to open-source tools where you can
- Host your own data when possible
- Learn basic digital literacy and teach others
- Support open-source projects — code, documentation, or funding

**As a community:**
- Start a community broadband initiative
- Create a local tech support cooperative
- Build a community mesh network
- Organize digital literacy workshops

**As a region:**
- Invest in community-owned broadband infrastructure
- Require open standards for government technology
- Fund digital literacy programs
- Support right-to-repair legislation

## Already Happening

This isn't theoretical. Around the world:
- Linux runs the majority of the world's servers and smartphones
- Guifi.net in Catalonia is the world's largest community mesh network with 35,000+ nodes
- Signal provides encrypted communication to millions without ads or tracking
- The Right to Repair movement has won legislation in multiple countries
- Chattanooga's municipal broadband offers gigabit speeds at affordable prices

The tools exist. The knowledge is shared. What's needed is communities choosing sovereignty over convenience.
